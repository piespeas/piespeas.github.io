Screenshare Guide (Staff Only)

0. Before you start

  • Make sure you meet your server's requirements to perform screenshares (age, trust, technical knowledge, neutrality, etc.).
  • Confirm the server rules on what counts as proof and what bans are allowed; you must follow their standards, not your own.
  • Ensure you can record the entire screenshare (e.g. OBS), because recording protects both you and the player and is a key self‑defence mechanism.
  • Check that ping and connection are reasonable for both sides to avoid technical issues during the SS.
  • Have your SS toolkit ready and organized (folder structure, shortcuts, and any batch files/scripts you are allowed to use).

More detail:

  • Keep a standard SS checklist open so you do not forget steps under pressure.
  • Test recording (audio, resolution, FPS) so footage is usable as evidence.
  • Use a consistent naming scheme for recordings/logs like Player_Server_YYYY-MM-DD_SS.mp4.
  • Ensure you have enough disk space for long recordings and exports.
  • Maintain a versioned toolkit (v1, v2…) so you know exactly what was used.
  • Before big events, run a self‑screenshare on your own PC to confirm everything works.

1. Ethical and procedural setup

  • Treat the SS as a targeted digital investigation, not a witch‑hunt; the goal is to demonstrate cheat usage or convincingly rule it out.
  • Never SS players you are directly playing against; ask another screensharer to handle those cases.
  • Do not allow player‑to‑player screenshares; only trusted staff/SS team members should screenshare.
  • Be transparent: explain you will follow a structured procedure and that everything is recorded.
  • Stay calm, professional, and neutral; do not provoke or mock the player.

More detail:

  • Start with something like:
    Staff: This is a formal screenshare; I'll follow our standard procedure and record everything for both your safety and mine.
  • Avoid early statements of guilt; say "we'll check your PC for anything against the rules" instead.
  • If the player is anxious, remind them cooperation and honesty help; you are not here to leak their private info.
  • If you are emotionally involved (you just fought them), hand the case off.
  • No harassment: no insults, threats, or "confess or longer ban" behaviour.
  • Follow specific policies for minors or players who clearly don't understand.

2. Technical setup with the player

  • Have the player join the official SS / support Discord made for screenshares, not random private DMs.
  • Use your approved remote‑access method (e.g. AnyDesk or Discord screenshare with control, depending on policy).
  • Confirm audio and video are working and recording captures everything.
  • Ask the player to close unrelated private windows if your policy requires it.
  • Confirm the Windows version and architecture (e.g. Windows 10/11, 64‑bit).

More detail:

  • Ensure they share the full desktop, not one window.
  • Make sure you can see taskbar, system tray, and current time.
  • Ask to disconnect extra displays if they could be used to hide stuff (if rules allow).
  • Confirm they are on the same Windows user that plays on the server.
  • Ask them to temporarily exit fullscreen games and show the desktop.
  • Note all drives (C:, D:, etc.), as cheats can be hidden on secondary drives.

3. Phase One – quick artifact and trace check

First Phase focuses on common, fast‑to‑check locations and artifacts.

Goals:

  • Quickly find obvious cheats/loaders/tools.
  • Gather early proof of execution (POE) indicators.

3.1 Prefetch analysis (WinPrefetchView / PECmd)

  1. Open WinPrefetchView.
  2. Sort by Last Run Time and check executables run around suspicious times.
  3. Look for:
    • Known clients/cheats.
    • Loader names.
    • Suspicious random‑looking names in weird paths.
  4. If it does not work properly, use PECmd:
    • Create a folder (e.g. C:\Prefetch2).
    • Copy relevant prefetch files into that folder.
    • Run PECmd on C:\Prefetch2 and review parsed output.

More detail:

  • Watch run counts; many recent runs near the report time are very suspicious.
  • Write down exact names and paths in a note file.
  • Compare times with server logs (when they were fighting or setting off alerts).
  • Watch for legit‑looking names in non‑standard directories (deep Temp, odd locations).
  • Confirm Minecraft/Java prefetch entries match the claimed launcher and location.
  • If there is prefetch for a loader but no files now, plan to dig into USN/deletion traces later.

3.2 Temp, Recent Items, Deleted Items, Macros

  1. Check key Temp directories (e.g. AppData\Local\Temp) for:
    • Extracted .zip/.rar contents.
    • Cheat‑like .exe files.
  2. Use Recent Items / Quick Access for:
    • Recently opened archives.
    • Recently opened .jar, .exe, .bat or loaders.
  3. Inspect the Recycle Bin for deleted cheat folders/archives/executables.
  4. Check common macro locations for:
    • Macro scripts.
    • Config files (.json, .xml, .ini) for clickers/ghost clients.

More detail:

  • Sort by Date Modified to see what changed near suspicious gameplay.
  • Look for archives downloaded, extracted, then deleted and redownloaded.
  • Macro names referencing CPS, clicker, macro, combo etc. are suspect.
  • Check if macros are bound to mouse/keyboard software that the player uses.
  • Compare timestamps of archives or macros with when the player started performing unusually well.
  • In Recycle Bin, look for fresh deletions (minutes/hours before SS).

3.3 Immediate PowerShell script

  1. Run your designated PowerShell SS script.
  2. Let it gather:
    • Recent execution data.
    • Key directory/file summaries.
  3. Open the output (often C:\ScreenShare\*.txt).
  4. Search for:
    • Client names.
    • Loader names.
    • Strange paths or hidden folders.

More detail:

  • Use the editor's search to jump through multiple cheat keywords quickly.
  • If timestamps exist, align them with suspicious play times.
  • Copy key lines into your notes for easy quoting later.
  • If script lists startup entries, cross‑check with Task Manager's Startup tab and Task Scheduler.

3.4 Quick process and folder inspection

  • Use Task Manager / System Informer to spot:
    • Suspicious processes.
    • Unknown overlays or injectors.
  • Check main Minecraft / client directories for obvious hacked clients (if your rules say presence matters).

More detail:

  • Look for multiple Java processes when only one is expected.
  • In System Informer, pay attention to unsigned or odd DLLs loaded into Minecraft or Java.
  • Confirm the launcher (vanilla, custom) matches their claim.
  • Check for recording/overlay apps known to be bundled with cheats.

3.4.1 Detailed check of javaw.exe with System Informer

Use this when the player is on Minecraft (or any Java‑based client) and you want to inspect the running Java process for suspicious behaviour.

Step 1 – Locate the correct javaw.exe process

  • Open System Informer on the player's PC.
  • Make sure Minecraft is running and they are in the same environment where they were suspected (e.g. on your server, same client).
  • In System Informer's Processes tab:
    • Click the column header "Name" to sort alphabetically if needed.
    • Look for javaw.exe (there can be more than one instance).
    • Right‑click each javaw.exe and select Properties.
    • In the Image or General tab, check:
      • The path (e.g. legitimate Java installation vs strange custom path).
      • The command line, if visible (should show the Minecraft launcher / arguments, not random suspicious flags).
  • Identify which javaw.exe corresponds to the active Minecraft instance by:
    • Looking at the one using the most CPU and memory while they move in‑game.
    • Optionally asking them to briefly alt‑tab or pause the game and seeing which process reacts.

Step 2 – Verify the javaw.exe path and integrity

  • From the Properties window of the suspected Minecraft javaw.exe:
    • Check that the file location is inside a normal Java or launcher directory (e.g. C:\Program Files\Java\... or a known launcher folder).
    • If the path is inside Temp, random folders, or a clearly custom directory, treat that as suspicious and note it.
    • Use the option to open file location from System Informer and visually inspect the folder:
      • Check for unusual DLLs or EXEs next to javaw.exe.
      • Look for names related to cheats, injectors, or loaders.

Step 3 – Inspect modules (DLLs) loaded into javaw.exe

  • Right‑click the active Minecraft javaw.exe in System Informer and choose Properties (if closed).
  • Go to the Modules tab (or equivalent):
    • Scroll through the list of loaded DLLs.
    • Look for:
      • Modules with random or obfuscated names (e.g. meaningless characters).
      • DLLs located in Temp or other non‑standard folders.
      • Known cheat or injection framework names.
    • If you find a suspicious module:
      • Right‑click the module and choose Properties or Open file location.
      • Note the full path, file name, and any version/company info shown.

Step 4 – Inspect handles and threads (advanced)

  • In the same javaw.exe Properties window, look at:
    • Threads tab:
      • Identify threads with unusual start addresses or belonging to unfamiliar modules.
      • Look for threads started from DLLs that you already flagged as suspicious.
    • Handles or equivalent views:
      • Check if javaw.exe is attached to strange device handles or other processes that don't match normal Minecraft behaviour.
  • Do not terminate threads or handles unless your server policy explicitly allows it; generally you are only observing, not changing the system.

Step 5 – Cross‑checking javaw.exe with other evidence

  • Take all information you saw in System Informer for javaw.exe and cross‑check with:
    • Prefetch:
      • Does the same suspicious path or name appear in Prefetch entries?
      • How often and how recently was it run?
    • LastActivityView:
      • Do you see the suspicious Java executable being launched around the time of the suspicious gameplay?
    • USN / OSForensics:
      • Is there file activity (create/modify/delete) around the suspicious Java folder while they were playing?
  • If you find a strange Java path, unfamiliar DLLs injected into javaw.exe, and matching activity in Prefetch/LastActivityView/USN around suspicious times, treat these as potential POE elements and add them to your 3‑POE reasoning.

Step 6 – Documenting your javaw.exe findings

  • In your SS notes, write a small section such as:
    • javaw.exe analysis:
    • Path used: [full path].
    • Modules of interest: [list suspicious DLLs with paths].
    • Related activity:
      • Prefetch / LastActivityView / USN references.
  • Take screenshots of System Informer windows (process list, modules, properties) if your server allows storing them as part of SS evidence.

3.5 Evaluate Phase One

  • If you already have strong, well‑documented indicators of executed cheats, you might be close to 3‑POE.
  • If nothing obvious appears and you looked carefully, move to Phase Two (either legit or heavy bypass).

More detail:

  • Pause and summarize to yourself:
    • "What strong evidence do I have?"
    • "What is still unclear?"
  • Tag the situation as:
    • Very likely clean.
    • Suspicious but not proven.
    • Strongly pointing to cheats (needs Phase Two to confirm).
  • Add this summary to your SS notes so reviewers see your reasoning.

4. Phase Two – deeper system investigation

Use this when Phase One is clean or inconclusive.

Goals:

  • Catch bypassers who cleaned surface traces.
  • Use overlapping sources (logs, tasks, forensic tools) to build or dismiss cheating.

4.1 Event Logs

  • Open Event Viewer and focus on key time windows.
  • Check Application and System logs for:
    • App starts/crashes.
    • Suspicious service behaviour.

More detail:

  • Look for unknown executables that start or crash near suspicious matches.
  • If players blame "PC crashes" for weird behaviour, check if logs support that claim.

4.2 Task Scheduler

  • Open Task Scheduler.
  • Look for:
    • Random or meaningless task names.
    • Tasks launching from odd folders.
    • Tasks that run at login or startup.

More detail:

  • Focus on tasks that run scripts or unknown .exe files from Temp or unusual directories.
  • Check triggers and actions of each suspicious task.
  • Look for recent creation/modification of tasks around suspicious sessions.

4.3 SearchEverything (or similar)

  • Use SearchEverything for:
    • Known cheat names.
    • Loader names.
    • Generic cheat and macro terms.
  • Open locations and inspect names, timestamps, and relevant content.

More detail:

  • Use multiple searches: client names, bypass names, generic terms.
  • Sort results by Date Modified to prioritise recent activity.
  • Try partial names and variants if cheats rebrand under similar patterns.

4.4 System Informer, Maceta, OSForensics, SherlockFinds

  • With System Informer:
    • Review processes/services/modules for unusual or injected elements.
  • With OSForensics / similar tools (including Maceta‑like tools, SherlockFinds):
    • Inspect recent file system activity.
    • Correlate file creation/modification to cheat suspicion timeframes.

More detail:

  • Sort by CPU/memory to spot unknown processes that stay active during gameplay.
  • Filter forensic views to last 24–72 hours.
  • Use internal search inside OSForensics‑type tools with your cheat keyword list.

4.5 USN Journal and LastActivityView

  • Use LastActivityView to see:
    • Timeline of executed programs/opened files.
    • Focus on just before and during suspicious gameplay.
  • Use USN analysis (via forensic tools) to:
    • Track creation/renaming/deletion of cheat folders and files.
    • Detect "download → run → delete" patterns.

More detail:

  • Typical suspicious sequence: download archive → extract → run exe → delete folder.
  • Rapid renaming of suspicious files is a red flag for evasive behaviour.
  • Combine LastActivityView, USN, Prefetch and server logs to build a unified timeline.

4.6 Browser and download traces (if allowed)

  • Check browser download history and Downloads folder for cheat archives/loaders.
  • Only check within your server's privacy rules.

More detail:

  • Compare download times with Prefetch / LastActivityView execution times.
  • Look for repeated downloads of similar files (updating cheats, re‑trying bypasses).
  • Note contradictions like "never downloaded anything" vs. clear cheat downloads.

4.7 Checking browser downloads (per browser)

Use these steps to properly review downloads per browser while respecting your server's privacy rules.

4.7.1 Google Chrome / Chromium‑based (Chrome, Edge, Brave, etc.)

  • Ask the player which browser they used to download anything related to Minecraft or mods.
  • Open the browser (Chrome, Edge, Brave, etc.) on their PC.
  • Press Ctrl + J to open the Downloads page.
  • Scroll through recent downloads and look for:
    • Archives like .zip, .rar.
    • Executables .exe, .jar, .bat.
    • File names or URLs that reference clients, injectors, macros, or loaders.
  • For any suspicious item:
    • Hover to see the original download URL if visible.
    • Click "Show in folder" (or similar) to open the file location if the file still exists.
    • Note the download date/time and the file path.
  • Cross‑check suspicious download times with:
    • When the player first joined your server or started playing suspiciously.
    • Prefetch / LastActivityView times for corresponding executables.

4.7.2 Firefox

  • Open Firefox on the player's PC.
  • Press Ctrl + J or click the downloads icon in the toolbar, then "Show all downloads".
  • Review the list for:
    • Cheat‑related file names, archives, or executables.
  • Right‑click a download and choose "Open Containing Folder" (if available) to jump to the file.
  • Note:
    • Download name.
    • Download date/time.
    • File location on disk.
  • Cross‑reference with:
    • Prefetch entries for that executable.
    • File creation/modification activity from USN/OSForensics.

4.7.3 Other browsers

  • Most browsers have a Downloads section accessible via:
    • A downloads icon in the toolbar.
    • Keyboard shortcuts like Ctrl + J or a "History > Downloads" menu.
  • Once in the downloads view:
    • Repeat the same logic: find archives/executables and note times and paths.

Important:

  • Stay inside the downloads area and the related Downloads folder unless your server's policy allows deeper browsing.
  • Use download timestamps as part of your POE timeline when they match execution events and suspicious gameplay.

5. Applying the 3‑POE (Proof of Execution) idea

  • Focus on Proof of Execution, not just presence.
  • A client sitting unused on disk (e.g. Wurst, last used a week ago) alone is not enough unless your server says otherwise.
  • Look for independent execution‑related traces:
    • Prefetch: cheat executable ran at the relevant time.
    • LastActivityView: cheat/loader launched.
    • USN/OSForensics: cheat folder created/modified, files changed during play.
  • Move towards a fail only when you have three independent POE items tied to a known cheat/loader and it fits your rules.

More detail:

  • Write your POE clearly in notes, for example:
    • POE 1: Prefetch shows client.exe ran at [time].
    • POE 2: LastActivityView shows client.exe opened at [time].
    • POE 3: USN shows client folder created & modified during that session.
  • If you have two extremely strong POEs, check how your rules treat that, but do not improvise beyond them.
  • Distinguish historical cheating from cheating on your server; rules might handle these differently.

5.1 Step‑by‑step using LastActivityView for POE

Use this to turn LastActivityView into clear POE entries you can write into your notes.

Step 1 – Open LastActivityView and set timeframe

  • Launch LastActivityView on the player's PC.
  • Let it load the full activity list.
  • If available, configure it to show a reasonable timeframe (for example, last 24–72 hours), focusing on when the player was active on your server.
  • Sort by Date/Time in descending order so the newest entries are at the top.

Step 2 – Filter by relevant activity types

  • Check the activity type column (for example: "Run .EXE file", "Open file", "Software installation", etc.).
  • Focus mainly on:
    • Program executions.
    • Recently opened executables and archives.
    • Actions related to known cheat directories.
  • Use the built‑in search (if available) to look for:
    • Known cheat names.
    • Loader names.
    • Keywords related to clients, macros, and injectors.

Step 3 – Match LastActivityView entries to suspicious files

  • When you see an entry for a suspicious executable or archive:
    • Note the exact time of execution/opening.
    • Note the full path of the file.
    • Check whether this file also appeared in:
      • Prefetch output (for execution).
      • Browser downloads (for original download).
      • USN/OSForensics (for creation/modification).
  • Check whether the LastActivityView execution time lines up with:
    • When they joined the server.
    • When they were recorded doing suspicious actions.

Step 4 – Build a LastActivityView‑based POE chain

  • For each suspicious file, try to build a chain like:
    • Download (seen in browser).
    • File activity (seen in USN/OSForensics).
    • Execution (seen in LastActivityView).
    • Runtime traces (seen in Prefetch).
  • When LastActivityView shows:
    • A cheat executable or loader being run just before/during suspicious gameplay, and
    • This event agrees with Prefetch and USN timelines,
    treat this execution entry as a strong candidate POE item.

Step 5 – Document LastActivityView entries in your notes

  • In your SS notes, create a "LastActivityView" subsection and log:
    • Date/time of each suspicious execution.
    • File path and file name.
    • Relationship to gameplay (for example, "3 minutes before fight clip").
  • For each POE candidate, explicitly write:
    • "POE (LastActivityView): [file] executed at [time], matching [clip / log time]."

Step 6 – Combine LastActivityView with other POE sources

  • Use LastActivityView execution entries alongside:
    • Prefetch entries for the same executable.
    • USN/OSForensics showing file creation or modification.
    • Browser downloads showing where it came from.
  • When you can point to three separate sources (for example, Downloads + LastActivityView + Prefetch) all confirming the same cheat executable was downloaded and executed around the suspicious time, you have a solid 3‑POE case.

6. Decision and documentation

  • Base your decision strictly on:
    • Server rules on ban‑worthy behaviour.
    • Your evidence and 3‑POE reasoning.
  • Document clearly:
    • Artifacts found (paths, names, outputs).
    • Why they show execution or why they don't.
    • Timestamps, screenshots and exported logs.
  • If evidence is insufficient:
    • Do not force a ban; leave it as "no sufficient evidence".
  • If evidence is sufficient:
    • Briefly tell the player (if allowed) what was found and what rule it breaks.
    • Forward everything (notes, logs, recording) to staff/higher‑ups.

More detail:

  • Use a standard format in staff channels, e.g.:
    • Player:
    • Date/Time:
    • SS Staff:
    • Summary:
    • Evidence list (POE 1/2/3):
    • Conclusion:
  • Always attach or link the full recording.
  • Mention any limitations (USN truncated, browser cleared, etc.) so reviewers know the context.

7. Behaviour, privacy, and safety

  • Respect privacy and data policies at all times.
  • Avoid opening personal folders (photos, documents, banking) unless rules and necessity require it.
  • Keep all collected data secure and only share with relevant staff.
  • Use recordings to protect against false accusations and for training.
  • Never share sensitive findings publicly or with random players.

More detail:

  • If you accidentally see obviously private content, move away immediately and avoid discussing details.
  • Use sanitized recordings for training so real private data is not shared.
  • Keep your own machine safe by only using trusted tools; avoid random downloads during SS work.

8. Phase 3 – heavy suspicion & edge cases

Use this when suspicion is strong but 3‑POE is not fully reached, or environment is very unusual.

8.1 Re‑checking contradictions

  • Compare player claims vs. findings:
    • Claimed no cheats vs. obvious cheat traces.
    • Claimed no downloads vs. download history.
  • Treat lies as signal, not as direct proof.
  • Write contradictions in notes with exact wording/time.

8.2 Timeline reconstruction

  • Build a simple timeline:
    • Report / clip time.
    • Download times.
    • Executable run times.
    • Creation/rename/delete events.
  • Look for repeating patterns like "download → run → Join server" across multiple days.

8.3 Handling cleaned machines

  • If everything looks "too clean":
    • Look for signs of cleaning tools or mass deletion.
    • Note "heavily cleaned before SS" with what hints that.
  • Follow server policy on how to treat suspicious pre‑SS cleaning.

8.4 When to stop

  • Do not dig forever; after a full procedure with no proof, accept that:
    • The player is either legit or using a bypass beyond your current method.
  • Without valid proof by server standards, you cannot justify a ban.
  • Mark the SS as "no sufficient evidence" and store notes.

9. Communication templates

9.1 Start of SS

Staff: Hey, this is a formal screenshare for [ServerName].
I'm going to follow our standard SS procedure and record everything for both your safety and mine.
Please cooperate fully and don't close anything unless I ask you to.

9.2 If player refuses or stalls

Staff: Refusing to cooperate or intentionally wasting time can be treated the same as failing a screenshare under our rules.
I'll give you one more chance to cooperate; otherwise I have to follow the server's SS policy.

9.3 When you find enough evidence

Staff: I've found several pieces of evidence showing that cheats were run on this machine that break our server rules.
I'm going to end the SS now, finalize my notes, and forward everything to the staff team.

9.4 When you don't have enough evidence

Staff: I'm not seeing enough here to prove cheating under our current SS rules.
I'll mark this SS as "no sufficient evidence."
This doesn't guarantee anything for the future, but for now I can't punish you based on this SS.

10. Common mistakes for new ScreenSharers

  • Tunnel vision on one tool
    • Only relying on a single viewer or folder.
    • Always cross‑check with multiple tools and sources.
  • Banning on presence, not execution
    • Banning just for an old client installed.
    • Focus on execution, unless rules say presence alone is enough.
  • Ignoring server rules
    • Applying personal standards instead of official policy.
    • Keep the SS and punishment policy open while working.
  • Poor note‑taking
    • Not writing down actions or findings.
    • Maintain a live notepad: times, tools, key artifacts, contradictions.
  • Over‑exploring private data
    • Opening irrelevant personal files or conversations.
    • Stick strictly to game‑/cheat‑related areas.

11. Suggested staff training workflow

  • Step 1 – Shadowing
    • New staff quietly watch experienced SS sessions.
  • Step 2 – Volunteer practice
    • Full SS runs on willing staff/friends to practice the checklist.
  • Step 3 – Mock "planted cheat" cases
    • Simulated suspects with known cheat setups to train detection.
  • Step 4 – Review sessions
    • Leads review recordings, giving feedback on missed steps or issues.
  • Step 5 – Approval
    • Only after multiple successful, reviewed SS runs should new staff SS real players alone.
